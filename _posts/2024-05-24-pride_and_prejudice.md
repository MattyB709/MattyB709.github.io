# Pride and Prejudice

Date finished: May 23, 2024

## Thoughts on the book

I enjoyed Pride and Prejudice much more than I expected. I knew it was famous and a classic work of literature, but I've never been particularly interested in romance, and after loving 100 years of solitude so much, I didn't expect to be that impressed. 

The first thing I noticed about the book was that it was really fun. Most of the classic works of literature I've read have really compelling themes and strong characters, which I find thought-provoking, but Pride and Prejudice is one of the first books I've read in a while that felt fully fun to read, start to finish. The book never feels like it slows down, and it all builds to both the romance between Elizabeth and Mr. Darcy and the other themes.

## The Romance

On its surface, the book is about a typical hate to love story that starts out with an overheard insult. Beyond this, though, the Jane Austen provides a surprisingly modern and sharp social commentary of class divisions and gender roles of the time. Elizabeth Bennet, the main character, comes from a poor family in an English society where a persons worth comes primarily from how they were born. Despite her low status, Elizabeth is unafraid to challenge those born with higher rank, and her wit and intelligence make her a strong protagonist.

To most people in England at this time, Elizabeth's character traits would have been undesirable in a woman, especially a poor woman, but they actually attract the attention of Mr. Darcy, an extremely rich man she meets quickly into the book. In a typical class-criticism book like this, I would expect the story to be that Mr. Darcy overcomes his prejudices about lower classes and finds that Elizabeth is perfect for him, but what I find most clever about Pride and Prejudice is that Elizabeth is prejudiced against him instead. Mr. Darcy is an awkward person who does not know how to talk to people he doesn't know, and as a result he comes off as prideful and unpleasant. The people of Elizabeth's town, including Elizabeth, all dislike him, and when Mr. Wickham, a more charming man, spreads negative lies about Mr. Darcy's character, Elizabeth and the townspeople readily believe him because they already don't like Mr. Darcy. 

The gradual change in Elizabeth's perception of Mr. Darcy as she learns about his actual integrity and kindness provides a more nuanced perspective of the class system. The book is not simply saying that the rich are bad and the poor are good; Elizabeth is actually in the wrong early in the book making so many negative assumptions about Mr. Darcy just because of her bad first impression (the book was originally supposed to be called First Impressions). Finding out more about Mr. Darcy's great character as he falls more in love with Elizabeth (especially after she brutally turns him down the first time he asks for her hand) is very satisfying, and throughout the book I always wanted to know and see more of Mr. Darcy.
## Society at the time

Outside of the compelling story of Elizabeth and Mr. Darcy, the book also criticizes the society at the time through heavy satire. The Bennet family consists of five daughters, and because of the practice of entailment, their house has to go to the next male heir in the family after the death of the girls' father, which turns out to be some distant cousin Mr. Collins. Mr. Collins is an extreme kiss-ass towards anyone with status and doesn't have enough intelligence to realize when people don't like him. He's generally considered a fool, which is funny to read, but makes the reality that the daughters will lose their family home to this man much more tragic. Their situation not only ridicules the practice of entailment, but also shows how limited opportunities were for women in this society, because the daughters' only options are to get married or face extreme poverty after the loss of their house. 

Jane Austen was also surprisingly modern in the way she makes fun of the people who were considered 'great' at the time: people that were born rich without doing any work of their own. This view of greatness is largely considered ridiculous now, but it was very prevalent when this book was written, and Jane Austen's satire is a bold choice. 

Overall, I would highly recommend the book. It's very engaging, funny, has great characters, and provides some very interesting social commentary that still holds today. I see now why it is one of the classics, and I will be watching the Keira Knightley movie soon.

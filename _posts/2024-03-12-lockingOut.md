### Why Rust is such a great programming language
Rust consistently has been the most beloved programming language by its users for the last few years, and with all the time I've spent programming in Rust for GT mentorship I'm starting to understand why. Rust is extremely strict in the way it lets you handle memory. Every object or piece of data can have only one owner. For example:
'''rust
let x1 = vec![1,2,3]; // Creates a simple vector of values
let x2 = x1; 
'''
In a language like Python, if you were to print 'x1' or 'x2', you would get '[1,2,3]'. But in Rust, setting 'x2' to be equal to 'x1' transfers ownership of the underlying data (the vector), meaning 'x1' no longer has any underlying data. This makes Rust much faster than Python, because it is not allocating memory for the same data over and over again. This idea of ownership is at the center of how Rust works, and its compiler is very strict in making sure your code follows all of its rules so that your code is safe and runs fast. This is especially important when you're working with devices that don't have a lot of memory, like small security cameras or other IoT devices. This level of control over how your memory is handled is why Dr. Mamouras uses Rust. I want to describe in more detail the way Rust works, but I don't have time before I leave, so I'll probably dedicate more time figuring out how to explain Rust simply for next week.

### Who Patrice Lumumba was
Poisonwood Bible has actually been getting interesting. The story parallels the Congo's rise to independence from Belgium, including the story of Patrice Lumumba, who was the Congo's first prime minister. The book describes him as being charismatic and a good speaker, and so I got interested and looked into it more. Turns out, the guy was a badass. He was one of the few people in the country who had the guts to speak out against how horrifying Belgian colonialism was. He supported pan-africanism and complete independence from Belgium, which put a target on his back. Almost immediately after Lumumba became prime minister of the independent Congo, the Belgians orchestrated the secession of the southern section of the Congo (which was the part that was most rich in minerals). With his country in crisis, Lumumba reached out the UN, France, and the United States for help, and they rejected him. With nowhere else to go, he got help from the soviets, and soon after, he was killed. The CIA has been accussed of helping in the plot to kill him, which leaves an ugly mark on U.S. history. I want to read more on the situation as I wait at the airport tomorrow. Reading his story also makes me want to read more about colonialism in South America, if I have time I also want to read [The economic development of Latin America and its principal problems](https://archivo.cepal.org/pdfs/cdPrebisch/002.pdf), which goes into imperialism in South America. I think it's important to spend time learning about areas outside of comp sci to be a better decision maker in general.
